# From Explanations to Evidence: A Survey of Verifiable Visual Reasoning with Multimodal Language Models

## Abstract

Multimodal language models can now produce long-form visual rationales, invoke tools, write executable code, and operate as interactive agents. Yet this progress has exposed a central weakness: intermediate reasoning is often persuasive without being trustworthy. Visual chains of thought may look coherent while remaining weakly grounded, hard to audit, and brittle under tool noise, interface changes, or distribution shift. This survey studies **verifiable visual reasoning** as the problem of exposing intermediate traces that can be inspected, checked, replayed, or falsified. We review a structured expert-synthesis corpus of **89 papers released between January 2024 and April 16, 2026** and organize them with a simple 2x2 framework defined by two questions: what form does the intermediate trace take, and what verification channel is available for checking it? This perspective yields four recurring regimes: textual reasoning without external verification, textual reasoning with tools, structured reasoning without tools, and executable reasoning with tool or environment feedback. We further argue that recent world-model-based agents form an orthogonal predictive axis rather than a fifth category, because they mainly differ in how they simulate future states before acting. Beyond taxonomy, we synthesize cross-quadrant design patterns, training strategies, and evaluation protocols that move beyond answer accuracy to measure step correctness, grounding quality, replayability, robustness, and cost. Our main conclusion is that verifiability is a system property shaped by the joint design of representation, verification channel, runtime budget, and deployment risk.

## 1 Introduction

Multimodal language models have moved rapidly from short-form visual question answering to long-horizon reasoning and agentic interaction. Recent systems can explain charts step by step, localize answer-relevant regions, browse webpages, operate desktop interfaces, call perception tools, write executable programs, and even imagine future GUI states before taking an action \citep{wei2022cot, yao2023react, yang2023mmreact, fan2024videoagent, suris2023vipergpt, li2025mobileworldbench}. This expansion has created a new evaluation problem: once a model exposes intermediate reasoning, users and downstream pipelines naturally read that trace as evidence for why the final answer should be trusted.

That inference is often unsafe. A model may produce a correct answer with an incorrect explanation, may call tools but ignore their outputs, may cite visual evidence without grounding to the relevant region, or may generate executable traces that optimize for successful execution while remaining unfaithful to the question. These are not isolated failures. They arise because multimodal reasoning combines ambiguous perception, brittle OCR and detection modules, interface grounding errors, partial observability, and long interaction loops. What appears to be "reasoning ability" is often a mixture of language priors, visual shortcuts, and weakly verified intermediate behavior.

This survey argues that the field should be analyzed through a shift from **explanations** to **evidence**. The question is not simply whether a system can narrate a chain of thought, but whether the intermediate trace exposes something that can be checked by a human, a verifier, a tool, or the environment itself. This perspective unifies several lines of work that are usually discussed separately: visual chain-of-thought prompting \citep{xu2025llava, shao2024visual}, tool-augmented agents \citep{castrejon2024hammr, song2026adareasoner, wang2025chartagent}, structured scene-graph or token-based reasoning \citep{mitra2024ccot, wang2024llavaSG, qin2025covt}, executable reasoning systems \citep{suris2023vipergpt, hong2025deepeyesv2, hou2025codev}, and the emerging world-model literature for web, GUI, and computer-use agents \citep{luo2025vimo, Gu2025WebDreamer, guan2026computerusingworldmodel}.

Our synthesis is organized around two orthogonal design decisions. The first asks **what representation the intermediate reasoning takes**: free-form text, a structured artifact such as a graph or token sequence, or executable code. The second asks **how that reasoning can be checked**: internally through format or consistency constraints, or externally through tools, execution, or environment feedback. Their combination yields a simple 2x2 matrix that captures most recent work while still leaving room for an orthogonal predictive axis formed by world-model-based agents.

This survey makes four contributions. First, we review a structured expert-curated corpus of 89 papers and map them into a verifiability-centered taxonomy. Second, we distill the main strengths, blind spots, and system trade-offs of the four regimes, rather than treating the literature as a flat citation list. Third, we synthesize cross-quadrant trends in training, design patterns, evaluation, and deployment. Fourth, we argue that world-model augmentation should be understood as a predictive layer that cuts across the representation-and-verification taxonomy rather than replacing it.

This survey is timely for two reasons. First, the field has now accumulated enough diversity that the same label of "visual reasoning" covers substantially different evidence interfaces: free-form verbal explanations, tool-mediated action traces, structured latent or symbolic states, executable programs, and predictive world-model rollouts. Without a common framework, these developments are easy to compare only within narrow subcommunities. Second, deployment pressure is increasing. Multimodal systems are no longer evaluated only as benchmark solvers; they are being integrated into assistants, browser agents, analytic workflows, and scientific pipelines. In these settings, an intermediate trace is valuable only if it helps a user or downstream system decide when the model is reliable and when it is not.

For this reason, the survey should be read not only as a taxonomy of papers, but also as a design guide. Researchers interested in new methods can use it to identify which representation and verification channel best match a target failure mode. Practitioners can use it to decide whether their application needs readable textual evidence, structured intermediate states, explicit tool grounding, executable replay, or some hybrid combination. Reviewers can also use the framework to ask a sharper question of each paper: what kind of evidence does the method actually surface, and what exactly can be checked about that evidence? Framed this way, the literature becomes more coherent, and the contribution of each new paper becomes easier to evaluate on scientific rather than stylistic grounds. At the same time, the paper is best understood as a structured synthesis of a rapidly moving corpus, not as a claim to exhaust every adjacent multimodal reasoning thread.

## 2 Scope, Definitions, and a Verifiability-Centered Framework

### 2.1 Scope and Corpus

Our corpus contains 89 papers released between **January 2024 and April 16, 2026**. We focus on work in which the model exposes nontrivial intermediate reasoning over visual inputs, tool outputs, environment states, or executable artifacts. The surveyed papers span visual question answering, chart and document understanding, video reasoning, web and GUI agents, embodied control, executable program synthesis, and emerging world-model-based agents. We exclude purely single-step perception systems, standard VQA models without explicit reasoning traces, and general text-only reasoning work except when it provides conceptual background for multimodal methods.

The corpus was constructed with a structured expert-synthesis protocol rather than a formal PRISMA-style meta-analysis. We began with five anchor papers chosen to seed the four quadrants and the executable branch, then expanded through repeated keyword and venue passes, reference snowballing, and a final targeted pass on world-model-based agents. In practice, this meant searching public paper sources such as arXiv, OpenReview, ACL/CVF venue pages, and project pages using threads including *visual chain-of-thought*, *multimodal reasoning*, *multimodal agents*, *web agents*, *GUI agents*, *process supervision*, *scene graphs*, *latent reasoning*, and *executable visual reasoning*. We retained papers when the exposed intermediate trace was central to the method rather than merely an optional explanation.

To make the corpus more reproducible, we applied three concrete filtering rules. First, a paper was included only if an intermediate artifact was both explicit and method-relevant: free-form text, structured state, tool trace, executable program, or predictive world-model rollout. Second, when a preprint and a later venue version described the same technical contribution, we merged them by technical identity and used the latest stable public metadata for year and venue. Third, we allowed a small number of pre-2024 precursors such as ReAct, MM-ReAct, and ViperGPT to remain only as historical context rather than as part of the counted 2024--2026 corpus.

The corpus is intentionally broad because verifiability failures often emerge at the interfaces between research areas. A paper on web agents may expose the same failure pattern as a paper on chart reasoning, even if their benchmarks and architectures differ. Likewise, a code-based visual reasoner and a structured latent reasoner may differ more in replayability than in headline accuracy. A survey organized only by application area would miss these connections. Table 2 summarizes the resulting corpus-construction protocol and final distribution.

| Corpus aspect | Protocol used in this survey |
| --- | --- |
| Time window | January 2024 to April 16, 2026 for counted papers; a few pre-2024 precursors kept as background only |
| Search process | Anchor seeding -> keyword and venue expansion -> citation snowballing -> targeted world-model pass |
| Inclusion rule | Paper must expose a nontrivial intermediate trace that is central to reasoning or acting |
| Exclusion rule | Pure perception, answer-only VQA, or text-only work without direct multimodal relevance |
| Deduplication rule | Merge preprint and venue versions by technical identity; prefer latest stable public metadata |
| Final corpus | 89 papers: 27 in Q1, 21 in Q2, 20 in Q3, and 21 in Q4 |

### 2.2 From Explanation Quality to Evidence Quality

The central distinction in this survey is between **explanation quality** and **evidence quality**. Explanation quality concerns whether a trace reads coherently to a human. Evidence quality concerns whether the trace can be checked, replayed, or falsified. A textual statement such as "the left bar is higher" may be plausible, but it is not directly machine-checkable. By contrast, a trace that includes a region proposal, tool output, rendered plot, or executable program exposes intermediate structure that can be inspected more concretely.

This distinction matters because correct final answers do not imply faithful intermediate reasoning. Prior work in NLP already emphasized that plausibility and faithfulness should not be conflated \citep{jacovi2020faithfully}. Multimodal systems intensify this problem because visual traces can be weakly grounded even when they sound specific. A model may mention the "upper-left icon" without actually localizing it, or may produce a correct chart answer while deriving it from the wrong bar. In agentic settings, the model may emit a plausible next-step description while clicking the wrong button or querying the wrong tool. Verifiability, therefore, is not about stylistic quality; it is about what intermediate evidence the system exposes and whether that evidence can be tested.

### 2.3 Working Definition of Verifiable Visual Reasoning

We use the term **verifiable visual reasoning** for multimodal reasoning that exposes intermediate artifacts with at least one explicit checking mechanism. Depending on the method, this mechanism may be schema validation, step-level scoring, self-consistency under perturbation, tool feedback, code execution, or environment interaction. This definition is operational rather than philosophical. It does not assume that any single verifier is perfect. Instead, it emphasizes that trustworthy reasoning depends on what evidence is surfaced and how that evidence can be challenged.

The definition also distinguishes verifiability from full correctness. A verified step can still be wrong if the verifier is weak, the trace omits crucial information, or the environment feedback is noisy. Nevertheless, exposing checkable intermediate states is materially better than exposing only a persuasive narrative. Throughout the survey, we therefore treat verifiability as a graded systems property rather than a binary label.

### 2.4 A 2x2 Framework

We organize the literature with two axes:

1. **Intermediate representation**: Is the reasoning trace primarily textual or structured?
2. **Verification channel**: Is the trace checked internally, or does the method rely on external tools, execution, or environment feedback?

This yields four regimes, summarized in Table 1. The framework is intentionally simple. Its purpose is not to force every paper into a rigid box, but to reveal why methods that appear superficially similar often differ fundamentally in auditability and deployment behavior.

| Quadrant | Intermediate trace | Primary verification channel | Representative works | Typical strength | Typical risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Q1 | Textual rationale | Internal consistency, process supervision, self-critique | LLaVA-CoT, Visual CoT, VisualPRM, R1-VL | Readable and easy to deploy | Weak external grounding |
| Q2 | Textual plan plus tool interaction | Tool outputs, observations, verifier modules | VideoAgent, AdaReasoner, ChartAgent, VISTA-R1 | Stronger factual grounding and broader capability | Tool misuse, latency, coordination failures |
| Q3 | Structured non-executable trace | Schema, constraints, alignment checks | CCoT, LLaVA-SG, CoVT, Artemis | Better machine checkability without runtime tools | Structure may still be incomplete or weakly grounded |
| Q4 | Structured executable trace | Code execution, environment feedback, tool I/O validation | ViperGPT, DeepEyesV2, CodeV, CodeDance | Highest replayability and step-level validation | Security, cost, cascading execution errors |

The framework also clarifies that verifiability is not monotonic in a single dimension. Q1 methods may be the most readable but often the least externally grounded. Q4 methods may be the most replayable but also the most expensive and operationally risky. Q2 methods gain factual grounding but can fail through poor tool selection or tool output misinterpretation. Q3 methods often provide the cleanest compromise when structure matters but unrestricted execution is undesirable.

To reduce assignment ambiguity, each paper receives a **primary quadrant** according to the dominant inference-time evidence interface rather than the training recipe alone. If the trace is mainly natural language and checking remains internal, we place the paper in Q1 even when lightweight spatial cues are present. If textual planning is coupled to observations returned by tools or environments, the paper enters Q2. If the trace is structured but not itself executed for correctness, it belongs to Q3. If the intermediate artifact is replayable and execution or rendered outcomes are part of the checking channel, it belongs to Q4. Hybrid papers therefore keep one primary label for reproducibility while their secondary affinities are discussed later as cross-quadrant compositions.

Table 2.5 provides a few borderline examples to make this rule operational. The goal is not to claim that these papers have no secondary affinities, but to show how the primary assignment rule behaves on cases a reviewer is most likely to question.

| Borderline paper | Why it could be confused | Primary label used here | Reason for primary assignment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

| LLaVA-CoT | Multi-stage template can look like structured reasoning | Q1 | The stages organize free text, but correctness is still judged mainly through internal textual consistency rather than structure-level checking |

### 2.5 Relation to Adjacent Survey Areas

Our framing overlaps with, but is distinct from, several adjacent survey topics. First, it overlaps with general surveys of multimodal large language models, which typically emphasize architectures, training data, benchmark coverage, and modality breadth \citep{yin2024surveymllm}. It also overlaps with reasoning-oriented MLLM surveys that catalog emerging reasoning tasks and capabilities \citep{wang2024mllmreasoningsurvey}. However, those reviews usually treat reasoning as a capability axis, whereas our focus is narrower and more system-facing: what intermediate artifacts are exposed, and what can actually be checked about them.

Second, the survey intersects with multimodal chain-of-thought surveys \citep{wang2025mcotsurvey}. Our departure is that we do not treat all exposed chains as equally evidential. A free-form rationale, a scene graph, a tool trajectory, and an executable program may all look like "reasoning traces," but they imply very different auditability guarantees. The central comparison in this paper is therefore not simply *which reasoning style exists*, but *what verification channel that style enables*.

Third, the survey intersects with work on tool-using and multimodal agents \citep{jiang2025rationalitysurvey}. Yet most agent surveys organize papers by application domain, planning style, or rationality framework, whereas our emphasis is on the relationship between textual planning and external evidence. This shift matters because many failures in multimodal agents come not from bad language modeling alone, but from weak grounding between plans and tool interactions.

Fourth, our perspective partially overlaps with faithful explanation and interpretability research. The difference is that we emphasize **operationally accessible evidence** rather than hidden internal mechanisms. A step is valuable in this survey not because it reveals the model's true internals in a mechanistic sense, but because it yields an artifact that a user, system, or environment can inspect or falsify.

### 2.6 Core Design Dimensions Beyond the 2x2 Matrix

The 2x2 framework is useful, but it is not the entire story. Several additional design dimensions recur across the literature.

The first is **granularity**. Some systems expose one high-level textual decomposition, while others expose dense step-by-step tool traces or low-level executable programs. Finer granularity improves error localization but increases cost and may create more opportunities for compounding mistakes. The second is **grounding precision**. A rationale that references "the left object" is different from one that provides an explicit box, crop, or tool return value. The third is **replayability**. Some structured traces can be inspected but not rerun, while executable traces can often be replayed deterministically. The fourth is **deployment risk**, which includes security, latency, and robustness to tool or environment shifts.

These dimensions explain why two methods in the same quadrant can still behave very differently. For example, a Q2 chart agent with a carefully designed evidence package is much easier to audit than a generic ReAct-style browser agent, even though both combine text with tool observations. Likewise, a Q3 scene-graph system and a Q3 latent reasoning system are both structured, but they differ sharply in interpretability and human inspectability. We will return to these cross-cutting dimensions throughout the survey.

With this framing in place, the next four sections examine the quadrants in sequence. The goal is not merely to catalog representative papers, but to show how each regime changes the evidential status of an intermediate trace, what kinds of errors become easier to detect, and what new operational costs are introduced in return.

## 3 Textual Reasoning Without External Verification

Quadrant I is the natural starting point for the literature because it preserves the interface that users and benchmark creators already understand best: natural language. Q1 methods retain textual reasoning as the dominant intermediate trace and therefore maximize readability, portability, and compatibility with standard language-model training recipes. For this reason, Q1 has been the most active route for extending chain-of-thought ideas into multimodal settings. Representative methods include stage-structured prompting and distillation in LLaVA-CoT \citep{xu2025llava}, region-focused two-turn reasoning in Visual CoT \citep{shao2024visual}, process reward models such as VisualPRM \citep{wang2025visualprm}, critic-based refinement in Critic-V \citep{zhang2025critic}, and RL-based visual reasoning systems such as R1-VL and Visionary-R1 \citep{zhang2025r1vl, xia2025visionaryr1}.

The main contribution of this literature is not simply that longer rationales help. Rather, Q1 methods progressively refine how textual reasoning is generated, supervised, and filtered. LLaVA-CoT and Visual CoT demonstrate that explicit staging or region-aware prompting can improve reasoning quality by forcing a separation between perception and downstream inference \citep{xu2025llava, shao2024visual}. R-CoT, VisReason, and MIRA further expose how reasoning quality depends on whether intermediate text reflects actual visual engagement rather than memorized language patterns \citep{deng2024rcot, li2025visreason, zhou2025mira}. These works collectively suggest that textual structure is useful, but only when the model is pressured to connect it to visual evidence rather than treating it as post-hoc narration.

Process supervision is the second major strand in Q1. VisualPRM and VRPRM show that step-level scoring can be trained in multimodal settings, enabling best-of-N selection or process-level evaluation rather than relying only on final answers \citep{wang2025visualprm, chen2025vrprm}. Critic-V, Sherlock, SCL, and LLaVA-Critic-R1 push further by treating correction and critique as first-class learning problems rather than assuming that a base VLM can self-correct reliably at inference time \citep{zhang2025critic, ro2025sherlock, he2025self, wang2025llavacriticr1}. This is an important conceptual shift. The question is no longer only how to generate a rationale, but how to learn whether a rationale is locally credible and how to improve it when it is not.

Reinforcement learning has recently entered this regime as well. OpenVLThinker, R1-VL, Visionary-R1, and GThinker show that RL can improve multimodal reasoning even when no external tools are used, provided that the reward discourages superficial shortcuts and encourages structured visual engagement \citep{deng2025openvlthinker, zhang2025r1vl, xia2025visionaryr1, zhan2025gthinker}. The strongest Q1 papers therefore do not treat text as a static explanation format. They treat it as a trainable reasoning interface whose quality depends on whether the model is rewarded for visual faithfulness rather than only for answer correctness.

Despite these gains, Q1 methods remain limited by the same structural weakness: their intermediate traces are still mostly claims in language. They may be more faithful than earlier free-form CoT, but they are rarely replayable and often only weakly grounded. This weakness appears in several ways. Models may produce correct answers with partially incorrect chains, may describe regions without precise localization, or may learn to satisfy format rewards without improving actual evidence use. SCL is especially important here because it provides a strong negative result: inference-time self-correction alone does not reliably improve multimodal reasoning unless the model has been explicitly trained for correction \citep{he2025self}. Journey Before Destination and related benchmark papers make a complementary point from the evaluation side: the visual quality and faithfulness of reasoning traces can diverge substantially from final answer accuracy \citep{uppaal2025journey, chen2024measuring}.

For practitioners, Q1 remains attractive when readability, low deployment friction, and broad benchmark coverage matter most. It is less attractive when the application requires fine-grained audit trails, exact grounding, or reproducible intermediate states. In those settings, Q1 often serves best as a warm-start stage rather than a final design. Its greatest value may be that it teaches the model to externalize a reasoning trace at all, which can then be strengthened through structured states, tools, or executable supervision in later stages. The natural next move, therefore, is not to abandon textual planning altogether, but to ask whether that planning can be anchored to observations returned by tools or environments.

## 4 Textual Reasoning with Tools and Agents

Quadrant II can be read as the field's first large-scale attempt to repair the central weakness of Q1 without giving up the flexibility of text. These methods keep textual planning as the main control language but augment it with external actions, observations, or verifier calls. This is the most direct multimodal extension of the ReAct paradigm \citep{yao2023react}. In these systems, the model plans in text, invokes tools or sub-agents, reads returned observations, and continues reasoning. This pattern has proved highly effective in video understanding, document and chart reasoning, web interaction, desktop automation, and general visual tool use \citep{yang2023mmreact, fan2024videoagent, castrejon2024hammr, he2024webvoyager, zheng2024seeact, gao2024assistgui, wang2025chartagent, lu2025vista}.

The Q2 literature has matured along three fronts. First, several works show that careful tool design and orchestration matter more than simply increasing the number of tools. VideoAgent and DoraemonGPT illustrate this clearly in video reasoning: both systems rely on memory and targeted retrieval, but DoraemonGPT goes further by combining symbolic memory with search over action trajectories \citep{fan2024videoagent, yang2024doraemongpt}. AdaReasoner and HAMMR show that hierarchical or adaptive tool selection can outperform monolithic reasoning, especially when tasks require different combinations of perception, retrieval, and calculation \citep{song2026adareasoner, castrejon2024hammr}. ChartAgent pushes this line further with a structured evidence package that consolidates intermediate tool outputs into a more auditable artifact \citep{wang2025chartagent}. The emerging pattern is that stronger Q2 systems do not merely add tools; they restructure the interface between text, perception, and evidence aggregation.

Second, agentic interaction exposes new bottlenecks that are not visible in answer-only benchmarks. WebVoyager and SeeAct demonstrate that grounding actions to the correct webpage element is often harder than generating a plausible next-step description \citep{he2024webvoyager, zheng2024seeact}. AssistGUI and OmniParser reinforce the same lesson in desktop and GUI settings: interface understanding and action grounding can dominate overall failure rates, even when language planning appears strong \citep{gao2024assistgui, lu2024omniparser}. In other words, the reasoning text may look competent while the actual grounding layer fails. This observation shifts attention from generic reasoning quality toward state representation, element localization, and robustness to layout or interface variation.

Third, Q2 makes clear that tool augmentation does not automatically guarantee faithful reasoning. The model can call the wrong tool, use correct tools on irrelevant regions, or misinterpret valid outputs. In this sense, Q2 relocates the problem from "reasoning without seeing" to "reasoning without properly using what was seen." This is why recent agentic work increasingly adds verifier modules, trajectory scoring, or policy learning. VISTA-R1 and OpenThinkIMG respond by training policies end to end in tool-interactive environments rather than relying purely on prompted tool use \citep{lu2025vista, su2025openthinkimg}. Sherlock Workflow adds another layer by studying where verifiers should be placed in agentic workflows and how verification can be overlapped with execution without excessive cost \citep{ro2025sherlock}. These systems collectively argue that the key research problem is not tool access alone, but tool **selection**, **integration**, and **auditability**.

The strengths of Q2 are therefore practical rather than purely theoretical. It offers stronger factual grounding than text-only methods, broader task capability than most structured-but-self-contained approaches, and lower operational burden than fully executable reasoning. At the same time, its costs are real: latency accumulates over tool calls, failures compound across steps, and security becomes a first-class concern in web and computer-use settings. Q2 is currently the most natural regime for open-world visual agents, but it requires careful system engineering if its intermediate traces are to count as trustworthy evidence rather than as decorative planning text. The broader lesson is that external interaction helps only when the resulting evidence is itself organized and checkable. That observation motivates the next regime, which seeks stronger intermediate structure without paying the full runtime price of tool use.

## 5 Structured Reasoning Without External Tools

Quadrant III asks a different question from Q2: can a system gain auditability by changing the *form* of its intermediate reasoning rather than by calling more external tools? Q3 methods improve verifiability by changing the representation of reasoning itself. Their intermediate traces are structured enough to support schema checks, alignment constraints, or replay-like inspection, but they remain largely self-contained at inference time. This regime includes scene graphs, structured visual tokens, table-like traces, spatial state descriptions, and latent continuous reasoning states \citep{mitra2024ccot, wang2024llavaSG, qin2025covt, tang2025artemis, hao2024coconut, pham2025mcout, ma2025lare}.

One major line in Q3 uses explicit symbolic or semi-symbolic visual structure. CCoT and LLaVA-SG show how scene-graph-style intermediates can separate object-level perception from relation-level reasoning \citep{mitra2024ccot, wang2024llavaSG}. G2 and Graph-of-Mark further illustrate that graph-like structure can act either as an internal reasoning representation or as a visual prompt that reorganizes what the model attends to \citep{yuan2025g2, frisoni2026gom}. Artemis uses structured spatial tokens such as label-and-box pairs, allowing supervision and validation to operate directly over localized reasoning states \citep{tang2025artemis}. These approaches provide better machine checkability than free-form text, especially when the target task depends on object relations, layouts, or grounded decompositions.

Another line pushes toward compact or continuous latent structure. Coconut and MCOUT replace long textual traces with continuous latent reasoning states, while LaRe, DMLR, and CoVT attempt to preserve visual grounding by injecting or interleaving visual information into those latent trajectories \citep{hao2024coconut, pham2025mcout, ma2025lare, liu2025dmlr, qin2025covt}. These methods are important because they reveal a different side of verifiability. Structure is not always synonymous with human readability. A compact latent trace may be more stable or efficient than textual CoT, but it can also become less interpretable. Q3 therefore contains its own internal spectrum, ranging from human-readable symbolic traces to dense continuous states that are only weakly inspectable.

This internal spectrum is one of the most useful lessons from Q3. It shows that "structured reasoning" is not a single design choice. At one end, explicit scene graphs and tables support direct human inspection and schema-level validation. At the other end, latent reasoning states may only support indirect tests, such as alignment consistency or downstream robustness. Both belong in Q3 because neither depends on external tool calls at inference time, but they imply very different auditability stories. For practitioners, this means that the move from Q1 to Q3 should be motivated by the *kind* of structure required by the task, not by the vague belief that any structure automatically improves trust.

The main value of Q3 is that it offers machine-checkable intermediates without full tool or code execution. For many applications, this is an attractive middle ground. The system can expose object relations, patch references, layout descriptions, or structured states that are easier to validate than text while avoiding the operational risks of open-ended code. At the same time, Q3 methods do not escape the grounding problem entirely. If the structure is predicted inaccurately, validation may only confirm that the trace is well formed, not that it is correct. In practice, Q3 works best when the intermediate representation is tightly aligned with task geometry, such as scene structure, table reasoning, spatial grounding, or compact visual tokens whose semantics are externally constrained. When tasks demand not only inspectable structure but also deterministic replay, however, the literature moves one step further into executable reasoning.

## 6 Structured and Executable Reasoning

Quadrant IV is where the survey's notion of evidence becomes most literal. These methods generate executable programs, sketches, symbolic controllers, or code-grounded tool traces whose intermediate states can be validated by running them. The modern lineage begins with ViperGPT, which established the now-standard idea that visual reasoning can be expressed as executable Python over modular vision functions \citep{suris2023vipergpt}. Later systems extend this idea in three directions: training executable traces more effectively, making execution more faithful, and broadening the kinds of executable artifacts that count as reasoning.

The first direction concerns training. ViReP and VDebugger address the cold-start problem for executable reasoning by using execution feedback, reinforced self-training, and debugging-style refinement rather than assuming a large human-labeled corpus of visual programs \citep{khan2024virep, wu2024vdebugger}. DeepEyesV2 makes this pipeline more explicit by combining cold-start trace construction, code execution, and web search before reinforcement learning is used to refine policy behavior \citep{hong2025deepeyesv2}. Visual-ARFT and MM-Zero then show that RL can be used not only to improve final accuracy but also to shape the executable behavior of the model itself, including what code it emits and whether that code produces valid intermediate evidence \citep{visualarft2025, li2026mmzero}. In other words, Q4 is no longer just about expressing reasoning as code; it is increasingly about *training code-producing policies to behave verifiably*.

The second direction concerns faithful tool use inside executable traces. CodeV is especially important because it shows that executable reasoning can still be unfaithful: a model may call tools on irrelevant regions or ignore tool outputs while preserving answer accuracy \citep{hou2025codev}. Its contribution is not merely another code-based pipeline, but a sharper definition of what faithful visual tool use should mean in Q4. CodeDance and CodeVision expand the design space by treating code as a flexible interface for composing tools and image operations rather than as a fixed solver template \citep{song2025codedance, guo2025codevision}. Visual Programmability and ARM2 add adaptivity, allowing the model to choose between direct reasoning and executable reasoning depending on the problem structure \citep{visualprogrammability2025, xie2025arm2}. These works suggest that the future of Q4 is unlikely to be "always write code." Instead, the stronger principle is to expose executable traces precisely where replayability and exact validation are most useful.

The third direction broadens what counts as execution. Visual Sketchpad treats drawing commands as a replayable visual reasoning trace \citep{hu2024visualsketchpad}. CodePlot-CoT uses plotting programs as visual chains of thought for mathematical reasoning, making the intermediate plot itself part of the evidence \citep{codeplotcot2025}. RECODE uses derendering programs to reconstruct diagrams or charts into executable forms \citep{shen2025recode}. VLAgent and Act-Observe-Rewrite connect symbolic or code-like traces to agents acting in external environments, including embodied control \citep{xu2025vlagent, actobserverewrite2026}. VLM Scientific Discovery carries the same logic into scientific workflows, where code produces visual outputs that a VLM can check as part of an iterative validation loop \citep{vlmscientificdiscovery2025}. Across these examples, Q4 is best understood as the regime in which the intermediate trace is no longer just a description of reasoning but a programmatic object that can be executed, debugged, and challenged.

Q4 offers the strongest replayability and the sharpest step-level error localization in the current literature. It also imposes the heaviest operational burden. Open-ended code generation raises security and sandboxing issues; multi-step execution increases cost and latency; and early trace errors can cascade through later steps. Reward hacking is also a real concern: a model may optimize for execution success or surface-level verifier approval without producing genuinely relevant evidence. For these reasons, Q4 is most compelling when the application genuinely benefits from deterministic replay, fine-grained auditing, or exact tool-grounded evidence. It is less attractive when runtime budgets are tight or when unrestricted execution cannot be safely sandboxed. Taken together, the four quadrants show that verifiability is not a ladder with a single top rung, but a set of trade-offs among readability, grounding, replayability, and cost.

## 7 Cross-Quadrant Comparison

Having reviewed the quadrants individually, we can now compare them as alternative evidence interfaces rather than as competing benchmark tricks. Once the literature is viewed through a verifiability lens, the four quadrants reveal a more nuanced trade-off structure than a simple ordering from weak to strong. Table 3 summarizes the most important distinctions.

| Property | Q1 Text-only | Q2 Text + Tools | Q3 Structured | Q4 Executable |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary evidence unit | Rationale sentence | Tool observation plus text | Schema-constrained state | Program, sketch, or executable state |
| Human readability | Highest | High | Medium to high | Medium |
| External grounding | Weak | Strong if tools are well used | Medium | Strong |
| Replayability | Low | Partial | Medium | Highest |
| Error localization | Low | Medium | Medium | High |
| Typical runtime cost | Low | Medium to high | Low to medium | High |
| Main deployment risk | Over-trusting plausible text | Tool misuse and interface brittleness | False confidence in incorrect structure | Security, latency, cascading execution failures |

Three comparative lessons follow from this table. First, verifiability is multidimensional. Human readability, external grounding, and replayability do not always move together. Q1 is readable but weakly grounded; Q4 is replayable but operationally heavy; Q3 often occupies the cleanest middle ground when structure can be aligned to the task. Second, the same final answer can arise from very different evidence pathways. A chart question, for example, may be solved with a textual explanation, a structured scene-like representation, a tool package that reads values, or a full executable plotting program. Third, the right quadrant is application dependent. There is no single best regime once deployment risk and audit needs are considered alongside raw capability.

This comparison also helps explain a recurring pattern in the literature: stronger methods often combine regimes over time. Q1 rationales are used as warm starts, Q2 tools provide external evidence during data collection, Q3 structures stabilize intermediate states, and Q4 execution provides high-confidence traces for difficult cases. In this sense, the quadrants are not only categories for comparing papers; they are also design primitives that can be composed across training and inference.

Another cross-quadrant lesson concerns where confidence should reside. Q1 and Q2 often place confidence in the readability of the trace and the apparent relevance of external observations. Q3 and Q4 place more confidence in the structure or replayability of the trace itself. Neither allocation is universally superior. In low-stakes settings, readable but weakly checkable traces may be sufficient. In high-stakes settings, opaque but executable traces may be preferable if they enable deterministic auditing. A mature survey therefore should not ask which quadrant is "best" in the abstract, but which source of confidence is most appropriate for the deployment scenario at hand.

## 8 World-Model-Augmented Visual Agents

The quadrant comparison also clarifies why recent world-model papers feel related to, but not reducible to, any single box in the taxonomy. Recent work on world models adds an important predictive dimension to visual agents. These methods are not best viewed as a fifth quadrant, because they can still operate with textual, structured, or executable intermediate states. The key novelty is instead *when* the model reasons about state transitions. Standard agents are reactive: they act, observe the result, and then update the plan. World-model-augmented agents attempt to simulate future observations or state summaries before acting.

The clearest concentration of this literature is in mobile and computer-use settings. MobileWorldBench studies action-conditioned semantic transition modeling for mobile interfaces, which is closest to a textual or semantic form of lookahead \citep{li2025mobileworldbench}. ViMo and MobileDreamer move toward richer visual or sketch-like predictions of future screens, bringing world modeling closer to Q3-style structured state simulation \citep{luo2025vimo, cao2026mobiledreamer}. Generative Visual Code Mobile World Models and Code2World push further by making future states renderable or executable, thereby bringing lookahead into the Q4 regime \citep{koh2026generativevisualcodemobile, zheng2026code2world}. On the web and desktop side, WebDreamer and Computer-Using World Model use simulated transitions to rank or filter actions before execution \citep{Gu2025WebDreamer, guan2026computerusingworldmodel}.

The conceptual significance of this line is that it shifts part of verification from **after acting** to **before acting**. In reactive agents, poor actions are detected only once the environment returns a bad observation. In world-model-based agents, low-value actions can sometimes be rejected through imagined rollouts. This predictive capability does not replace the taxonomy developed above. Rather, it sits on top of it. A world model can predict in language, in a structured latent state, or in executable code. The more useful claim is therefore that world modeling is an orthogonal axis from reactive to predictive reasoning, not that it supersedes the representation-and-verification design space.

At the same time, world models inherit the same risks that govern the four quadrants. A semantic world model may hallucinate transitions. A structured world model may be internally consistent but miss crucial visual details. An executable world model may provide stronger replayability but also increase computational and implementation burden. For this reason, the most useful way to compare world-model agents is not "do they use a world model or not," but "what kind of future state do they simulate, how is that simulation checked, and how is it integrated with the acting policy?" Once stated this way, the literature points naturally toward a broader design question: which reusable patterns make verifiable multimodal systems work well across quadrants and across predictive versus reactive settings?

## 9 Design Patterns for Verifiable Multimodal Systems

One benefit of surveying the literature through a common framework is that it reveals a set of recurring **system design patterns**. These patterns are not tied to any single quadrant. Instead, they describe how strong systems package evidence, control runtime cost, and decide when to escalate from cheap but weak checks to expensive but stronger ones. They are useful precisely because they explain why superficially different papers often converge on similar engineering choices once verifiability becomes a first-class objective.

The first recurring pattern is **explicit evidence packaging**. Stronger systems do not leave intermediate evidence scattered across raw text, tool logs, and latent state. They package it into a compact, queryable interface that later reasoning stages can consume. ChartAgent's evidence package is an obvious example in the tool-using regime \citep{wang2025chartagent}. DoraemonGPT's symbolic memory plays a related role for long video understanding \citep{yang2024doraemongpt}. Scene-graph methods in Q3 achieve the same effect through structured representations, while executable systems achieve it through code, sketches, or renderable traces. The design lesson is that verifiability improves when intermediate evidence is not merely produced, but *organized*.

The second pattern is **separating acting from auditing**. Many failures in multimodal systems come from conflating the policy that proposes an action with the mechanism that checks whether the action was appropriate. Sherlock Workflow makes this separation explicit with verifier placement and speculative execution \citep{ro2025sherlock}. Critic-V and LLaVA-Critic-R1 instantiate it in the textual regime by training critique models or unified critic-policy systems \citep{zhang2025critic, wang2025llavacriticr1}. CodeV instantiates it inside executable reasoning by distinguishing tool invocation from the process-level assessment of whether the tool was used faithfully \citep{hou2025codev}. This separation matters because the same model that proposes a step is often the least reliable judge of whether that step is evidentially sound.

The third pattern is **progressive verification**. Not every step needs the strongest available checker. A practical multimodal system often uses a staged budget: cheap textual or structural checks first, stronger tool-based or executable checks only when uncertainty remains. This pattern appears implicitly in many papers even when it is not named as such. Q1 systems use self-consistency, process reward models, or critique reranking before escalating to more expensive sampling \citep{wang2025visualprm, chen2025vrprm}. Q2 agents often retrieve or ground before invoking specialist tools. Q4 systems may execute lightweight programs first and only invoke costly refinement loops when the initial trace fails. World-model agents similarly use imagined rollouts to prune poor actions before paying the cost of real environment interaction. The broader lesson is that verifiability should be budget-aware from the start.

The fourth pattern is **queryable memory and state abstraction**. In long-horizon settings, verifiability depends not only on local evidence but also on how previous evidence is stored. VideoAgent, DoraemonGPT, and long-horizon agents succeed in part because they do not repeatedly reason over raw history; they construct state abstractions that can be retrieved or queried \citep{fan2024videoagent, yang2024doraemongpt}. Structured memory is equally important in Q3, where scene graphs or patch states act as compressed, inspectable summaries. In world-model agents, future-state prediction itself becomes a form of structured memory over possible next observations. A useful design principle here is that memory should not be a hidden cache alone; it should be an inspectable substrate for later reasoning.

The fifth pattern is **hybrid traces rather than single-mode traces**. The most robust systems increasingly mix modalities of reasoning instead of committing to one. A textual plan may call a tool whose output is summarized into a structured state; a structured state may trigger an executable subroutine; a world model may provide a predicted next state that is then validated by actual tool feedback. This hybridization is not a sign that the taxonomy is failing. On the contrary, it shows that the taxonomy identifies reusable components that strong systems can compose. For system builders, the design question is therefore not "which single quadrant is best," but "which combination of evidence interfaces best matches the task and risk profile?"

These patterns suggest that the future of trustworthy multimodal reasoning will likely be **compositional**. Pure Q1, Q2, Q3, or Q4 systems remain valuable as research probes, but deployed systems may increasingly combine them. A reasonable architecture might begin with textual decomposition, use structured state to maintain memory, invoke tools for external grounding, and escalate to executable traces only for high-stakes subproblems. The literature already points in this direction, and the most promising next step is to study these compositions explicitly rather than only comparing monolithic systems.

This compositional view also changes how later sections should be read. Training, evaluation, failure analysis, and deployment are not separate from the taxonomy; they are the mechanisms that determine whether a chosen composition actually yields trustworthy evidence in practice. The next sections therefore shift from *what kinds of traces exist* to *how those traces are learned, measured, broken, and deployed*.

Table 6 summarizes the design patterns that recur most clearly across the survey.

| Pattern | Core idea | Most visible in | Main benefit | Main risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Evidence packaging | Consolidate intermediate evidence into a queryable artifact | ChartAgent, DoraemonGPT, scene-graph methods | Improves auditability and reuse | Packaged evidence may omit critical detail |
| Acting-auditing separation | Decouple proposing a step from checking it | Critic-V, Sherlock Workflow, CodeV | Reduces self-confirming errors | Auditor quality becomes a bottleneck |
| Progressive verification | Escalate from cheap checks to stronger checks only when needed | PRM pipelines, tool agents, world models | Better cost-quality trade-off | Hard to tune escalation thresholds |
| Queryable memory | Store evidence in structured retrievable form | Video agents, Q3 methods, world models | Supports long-horizon reasoning | Compression can erase relevant evidence |
| Hybrid traces | Combine text, structure, tools, and execution | Stronger recent agents across quadrants | Matches evidence form to subproblem | Adds system complexity |

## 10 Training Multimodal Systems for Verifiability

Across the four regimes, the literature reveals a consistent training progression. Put differently, once a paper chooses an evidence interface, it must still solve the separate problem of teaching the model to use that interface faithfully. The earliest stage is supervised distillation of longer or more structured traces, often generated by stronger models or curated pipelines. LLaVA-CoT, Visual CoT, and related works use this strategy to expose staged reasoning, region focus, or richer supervision \citep{xu2025llava, shao2024visual}. This stage is valuable because it teaches the model to *produce* an intermediate trace at all. By itself, however, it does not ensure that the trace is reliable.

The next stage adds process-level evaluation. VisualPRM and VRPRM explicitly score intermediate steps \citep{wang2025visualprm, chen2025vrprm}. Critic-V, Sherlock, SCL, and LLaVA-Critic-R1 use critiques, preferences, or verifier-style supervision to shape reasoning quality beyond outcome correctness \citep{zhang2025critic, ro2025sherlock, he2025self, wang2025llavacriticr1}. These methods are important because they redefine training targets: the model is no longer rewarded only for answering correctly, but also for exposing an intermediate process that can be judged. A strong lesson from this stage is that multimodal self-correction is not a free emergent property; it must usually be trained explicitly.

The final stage uses RL or RLVR-style recipes to optimize verifiable behavior directly. R1-VL, Visionary-R1, OpenVLThinker, and GThinker show that reinforcement learning can improve textual multimodal reasoning when the reward includes process structure or anti-shortcut pressures \citep{zhang2025r1vl, xia2025visionaryr1, deng2025openvlthinker, zhan2025gthinker}. In tool-using and executable systems, the same principle becomes even more consequential. VISTA-R1 and OpenThinkIMG learn tool-use policies in interactive environments \citep{lu2025vista, su2025openthinkimg}. DeepEyesV2, Visual-ARFT, CodeV, and MM-Zero use execution feedback, tool I/O, or self-generated tasks to optimize the quality of executable intermediate behavior \citep{hong2025deepeyesv2, visualarft2025, hou2025codev, li2026mmzero}. Taken together, these results suggest that verifiability is not a byproduct of stronger base models. It must be trained explicitly, with objectives that reward grounded intermediate behavior rather than only correct answers.

Two broader patterns are worth highlighting. First, **cold-start plus RL** appears repeatedly across quadrants. Systems often need a supervised phase to expose useful intermediate behaviors before RL can refine them. Second, **verifier quality becomes a bottleneck**. Once training depends on process rewards, critiques, or tool-based scoring, the paper is effectively only as strong as the supervision channel used to define "good" reasoning. This means that future progress in verifiable visual reasoning will likely depend as much on better verifiers and process datasets as on better base VLMs. That dependency also explains why evaluation has become more demanding: if training optimizes intermediate evidence, then evaluation must measure that evidence directly rather than inferring quality from final answers alone.

## 11 Evaluating Verifiable Visual Reasoning

A survey centered on verifiability cannot rely on final-answer accuracy alone. Once intermediate traces become training targets and deployment artifacts, they must also become first-class evaluation objects. The literature now supports at least five complementary evaluation dimensions, summarized in Table 4.

| Dimension | Core question | Representative methods |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Step correctness | Are intermediate steps locally correct? | VisualPRM, VRPRM, R1-VL |
| Grounding quality | Does the trace attend to the right evidence? | Visual CoT, PatchCue, CodeV |
| Replayability | Can the same trace be rerun or reconstructed? | ViperGPT, Visual Sketchpad, RECODE |
| Robustness | Does reasoning survive perturbations or tool noise? | Visionary-R1, CodeVision, world-model agents |
| Cost and latency | What is the computational price of verification? | WebVoyager, ChartAgent, CodeDance, Sherlock Workflow |

Several patterns emerge from these metrics. First, process-level evaluation is now a serious research topic rather than an auxiliary diagnostic. VisualPRM, VRPRM, and R1-VL explicitly measure or reward step quality \citep{wang2025visualprm, chen2025vrprm, zhang2025r1vl}. Second, grounding quality is increasingly treated as independent from answer accuracy, especially in studies of visual shortcuts, region attribution, and unfaithful tool use \citep{shao2024visual, qi2026patchcue, hou2025codev}. Third, replayability matters most in Q3 and Q4, where intermediate states can be reconstructed or executed. Finally, cost must be treated as part of the evaluation target, because many of the most verifiable systems incur substantial latency through multi-step interaction or execution.

The benchmark landscape remains fragmented. Some datasets target visual CoT and reasoning quality, others target tool use, long-horizon interaction, or program execution, and few benchmarks compare all four regimes under consistent notions of grounding and verification. This fragmentation explains why papers often report strong gains within one subcommunity while remaining hard to compare across the broader design space. A major need for the next generation of benchmarks is therefore *cross-regime evaluation*: the same task should ideally support textual, structured, tool-based, and executable solutions, with metrics that reveal what each regime gains and loses.

Table 5 turns this observation into a compact survey artifact by mapping each regime to its most common benchmark families, typical evidence-facing metrics, and recurring blind spots. The point is not that every cited paper reports every metric below, but that the literature in each regime tends to converge on these measurement habits. This makes two asymmetries immediately visible: Q1 and Q2 are still often judged by answer accuracy plus partial auxiliary metrics, while Q3 and Q4 more naturally support replayability and step-level audit metrics.

| Regime | Benchmark families | Final-task metrics | Evidence-facing metrics | Frequent gap |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Q1 | Visual CoT, VisualProcessBench, MIRA, VQA/math/science | Accuracy, pass@1, best-of-N | PRM step scores, human step labels, box/cue agreement | Perturbation-based grounding checks and cost reporting |
| Q2 | Web and GUI agents, chart/document agents, long-video agents | Success rate, completion rate, accuracy | Tool-call correctness, action grounding, trajectory checks, evidence packages | Standard tool-faithfulness metrics across environments |
| Q3 | Scene graphs, tables, latent or token-based structured reasoning | Accuracy, completion, retrieval/generation quality | Schema validity, structural alignment, relation consistency, patch/token alignment | Human-auditable metrics for opaque latent traces |
| Q4 | Program-aided VQA, derendering, executable math/geometry, code agents | Accuracy, execution success, completion | Re-execution success, render equivalence, tool-I/O faithfulness, debugger signals | Security-aware and budget-normalized comparisons |

A practical evaluation protocol should therefore answer at least four questions. Did the system solve the task? What evidence did it expose? Could that evidence be replayed or checked independently? And what was the cost of obtaining it? Without all four, evaluation risks overestimating methods that merely look reasoning-heavy. This is especially important for agentic systems, where answer correctness alone can mask brittle interface grounding, unsafe tool use, or excessive runtime overhead.

An especially important future direction is **claim-evidence alignment evaluation**. Surveying the literature makes it clear that papers often claim gains in faithfulness, grounding, or verifiability while reporting experiments that still mostly track answer accuracy. Future benchmarks should therefore require authors to specify which intermediate property is being improved and how that property is measured independently of the final answer. For example, if a method claims better tool grounding, the benchmark should include metrics over tool selection and tool argument relevance. If a method claims better replayability, the benchmark should test whether intermediate traces can be re-executed under controlled conditions. This style of evaluation would make the field much more resistant to performance gains that arise from scaling or prompt engineering but leave the underlying trust problem unchanged. It also creates a direct bridge to failure analysis, because once claim-evidence alignment is explicit, the field can state more precisely where each method still breaks.

## 12 Failure Modes Across the Stack

One advantage of a verifiability-centered survey is that it reveals recurring failure patterns that cut across applications and model families. These failures are easy to miss when papers are organized only by benchmark or domain. In practice, however, many ostensibly different systems fail for closely related reasons. They are best understood as failures of the evidence pathway rather than as isolated model mistakes.

The first recurring failure is **evidence laundering through plausible language**. This is most obvious in Q1, where a model can produce a highly readable rationale with little hard evidence behind it. But the same pattern appears in Q2 and Q4 as well. A tool-using agent may generate a text explanation that makes the tool interaction appear more relevant than it was. An executable agent may wrap a weak or irrelevant tool call in code that looks principled while still failing to ground to the right region. The common problem is that the textual narrative can dominate user perception of reliability even when the underlying evidence pathway is weak. This is why answer correctness and explanation plausibility are insufficient as standalone evaluation targets \citep{jacovi2020faithfully, he2025self, hou2025codev}.

The second recurring failure is **interface grounding failure**. In multimodal systems, reasoning quality often depends on whether the model can correctly bind language to a visual region, a GUI element, a tool argument, or a symbolic variable. WebVoyager and SeeAct show this vividly for browser agents \citep{he2024webvoyager, zheng2024seeact}. Chart and document systems face similar problems when OCR regions, axis labels, or value references are slightly misaligned. Even structured and executable methods are not immune: a program can be syntactically valid and fully replayable while operating on the wrong crop, patch, or object index. In other words, better reasoning machinery cannot compensate for a broken grounding interface.

The third recurring failure is **verifier mismatch**. Once a system relies on process reward models, critic models, or execution feedback, the verifier becomes part of the method. If the verifier scores superficial properties rather than relevant evidence use, the model can learn to optimize the verifier instead of the task. This problem appears in PRM-style training, in tool-use reward design, and in executable reasoning where a program's success criterion may be only weakly correlated with faithful evidence use. The risk is highest when the paper treats the verifier as a black box rather than as a fallible component with its own assumptions and blind spots. A mature view of verifiable reasoning therefore requires evaluating the verifier, not only the generator.

The fourth recurring failure is **budget-induced degradation**. Many systems are trustworthy only under generous compute or interaction budgets. A text-only model may require best-of-N sampling or PRM reranking. A tool-using agent may require many sequential calls before it grounds correctly. A world-model agent may need rollouts over multiple candidate actions. An executable reasoner may need iterative debugging or self-refinement. Under realistic latency or cost constraints, the same method can collapse back to a much less verifiable mode of operation. This is why runtime budget should be understood as part of method semantics rather than as a secondary implementation detail.

Viewed together, these failures suggest a practical principle: **the intermediate trace is only as trustworthy as the weakest interface that produced or checked it**. A faithful-looking rationale can hide weak visual grounding. A structured state can hide missing semantics. A tool trace can hide bad tool selection. A successful program can hide irrelevant evidence use. This principle also explains why stronger papers increasingly combine multiple forms of checking, such as process supervision plus region cues, tool calls plus verifier placement, or executable traces plus dense tool-I/O rewards. The field is moving toward systems that cross-check intermediate evidence through multiple channels because no single channel is reliable enough on its own. Once these failure modes are made explicit, the deployment consequences of each quadrant also become easier to state.

## 13 Applications and Deployment Regimes

The survey perspective becomes most concrete when tied to deployment scenarios. Different applications require different evidence interfaces, and the same method can look attractive or inadequate depending on who must trust its intermediate trace.

In **open-world web and GUI automation**, Q2 appears to be the most common practical default in the current corpus. These tasks require acting over changing interfaces, using OCR or grounding tools, and integrating dynamic observations. Purely structured or executable methods can help, but a moderate amount of textual planning remains useful for high-level control. The main challenge is not only competence, but safe and robust grounding under interface shifts and prompt-injection-like failures \citep{he2024webvoyager, zheng2024seeact, gao2024assistgui}.

In **chart, document, and visual analytic tasks**, all four regimes are viable, and the choice depends on how exact the evidence must be. Q1 may suffice for low-stakes explanation. Q2 becomes attractive when OCR, parsing, or retrieval tools materially improve fidelity. Q3 often works well when structured visual abstractions such as tables, patch references, or scene-like tokens align closely with the problem. Q4 becomes especially attractive when precise numerical extraction or reproducible derivation is required, as in derendering or chart-to-code pipelines \citep{wang2025chartagent, qin2025covt, shen2025recode, visualprogrammability2025}.

In **scientific and safety-critical workflows**, Q4 is often the most conceptually aligned regime because it exposes the strongest replayability and step-level validation. Scientific code analysis, experiment validation, and embodied control all benefit from executable traces that can be rerun and audited. However, these settings also make Q4's risks more serious: sandboxing, error propagation, and verifier reliability cannot be treated as implementation details \citep{hong2025deepeyesv2, actobserverewrite2026, vlmscientificdiscovery2025}. In many realistic systems, this motivates hybrid designs in which Q1 or Q2 handles broad reasoning while Q4 is reserved for critical verification steps.

In **video and long-horizon embodied tasks**, the trade-off is slightly different. These systems must integrate evidence over time, which makes memory design and action granularity central. Text-only methods often struggle because they compress too much visual evidence into verbal summaries. Tool-using agents help by retrieving clips, trajectories, or symbolic memories, while world-model approaches promise to improve planning by simulating future states before action. Yet the same long horizon also magnifies compounding error, making partial verification less reliable. Here, many of the stronger designs are hybrid: textual planning for high-level decomposition, tools for retrieval and perception, and more structured or executable checks at key decision points.

These deployment cases reinforce the core theme of the survey: no single quadrant dominates universally. Instead, the right regime depends on the application's tolerance for false confidence, runtime cost, security exposure, and the level of auditability required by users or regulators. Table 7 summarizes this deployment-oriented view.

| Deployment setting | Often-effective regime | Why | Main caution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| General-purpose assistant with visual explanation | Q1 | Readable, cheap, easy to deploy | Users may over-trust plausible text |
| Browser or GUI agent | Q2 | Needs external tools and environment feedback | Grounding and security failures dominate |
| Structured visual analytics | Q3 or Q2 | Structure helps without full execution | Validation may only check format, not truth |
| Scientific or code-mediated workflow | Q4 | Replayability and exact checking matter | Sandboxing and cost become first-class issues |
| Long-horizon interactive agent | Q2 plus world model | Tool grounding and predictive planning both matter | Errors compound across time and rollouts |

## 14 Open Challenges and Design Recommendations

The deployment discussion points directly to the field's unfinished agenda. The literature reviewed here suggests that the next phase of multimodal reasoning research will be shaped less by raw capability and more by how systems expose and validate intermediate evidence. Four challenges are especially consequential.

First, **robust grounding remains unresolved**. Textual explanations can still drift away from evidence, tool-using agents can still select the wrong tool or the wrong region, structured representations can still be internally consistent but visually wrong, and executable agents can still optimize for execution success without faithful evidence use. Verifiability therefore cannot be reduced to a single interface choice. It requires aligned supervision, task-appropriate intermediate states, and evaluation that explicitly checks grounding quality.

Second, **verifier quality is a bottleneck**. Once training depends on PRMs, critic models, tool outputs, or execution signals, the system inherits the weaknesses of those supervision channels. A weak verifier can make a reasoning trace look rigorous while preserving the same underlying shortcut behavior. Much of the future difficulty in this field will therefore lie not in generating richer traces, but in defining better processes for checking them.

Third, **operational constraints are now part of method design**. Tool use introduces latency and coordination failures. Open-ended execution introduces sandboxing and security risks. World models introduce rollout bias and prediction errors. A professional survey of this field should therefore resist the temptation to rank methods on a single axis from weak to strong. In practice, the right design depends on the application's tolerance for cost, risk, and false confidence.

Fourth, **benchmarking remains too siloed**. Q1 papers often optimize reasoning quality in answer-centric evaluation, Q2 papers optimize task completion in interactive environments, Q3 papers optimize internal structure quality, and Q4 papers optimize executable correctness. These are all valuable, but they make it hard to answer a simple question: which design yields the most trustworthy reasoning under a fixed budget? Better shared benchmarks and reporting standards are needed to make that comparison meaningful.

### 14.1 Standardized Trace Schemas

A major obstacle to cumulative progress is the lack of shared trace formats across papers. Textual traces, tool logs, scene graphs, executable code, and world-model rollouts are usually serialized in ad hoc formats tied to one benchmark or codebase. This makes it difficult to compare systems, reuse evaluators, or benchmark replayability under common conditions. A useful research direction is to define minimally expressive but shared schemas for multimodal reasoning traces: fields for visual references, actions, tool arguments, returned evidence, confidence, and failure states. Such schemas would not solve faithfulness by themselves, but they would make intermediate reasoning easier to evaluate and exchange across systems.

### 14.2 Robustness Under Distribution Shift and Adversarial Inputs

Verifiability is most valuable when the environment changes, yet that is also where current systems remain fragile. Web agents encounter prompt injection and layout shifts. Chart and document systems encounter OCR noise, low resolution, or unusual templates. World models encounter transition shift when deployed on apps or interfaces unlike those seen during training. Executable systems encounter API changes, tool version mismatches, and sandbox restrictions. Future work should therefore evaluate verifiable reasoning not only on clean held-out tasks, but also under perturbations that specifically target the intermediate evidence pathway. A system that remains accurate only when the evidence interface is clean is not yet a trustworthy system.

### 14.3 Verification Under Realistic Budgets

Many proposed solutions implicitly assume generous inference budgets. Best-of-N textual sampling, repeated verifier calls, multi-tool trajectories, iterative debugging, and model-based rollouts can all improve verifiability, but only if enough budget is available to use them. A key open question is how to design reasoning systems that degrade gracefully when budget is constrained. This suggests a research agenda around budget-aware verification policies, early-exit mechanisms, and selective escalation strategies that preserve the most important evidence checks even under strict latency limits. Without this line of work, many of the strongest survey ideas will remain research prototypes rather than deployable systems.

### 14.4 Human-AI Collaborative Auditing

Another underdeveloped area is the interface between machine verifiers and human reviewers. In many realistic settings, full automation is neither possible nor desirable. What matters is that the model surfaces the right evidence for a human to inspect. Q1 traces are easy to read but often weakly grounded; Q4 traces are replayable but may be too technical for many users. Future systems should therefore optimize not only for internal or automatic verification, but also for **auditable handoff**: what evidence package should be shown to a clinician, scientist, analyst, or operator when the model is uncertain? This question bridges interpretability, UX, and multimodal reasoning, and deserves more attention than it has received so far.

### 14.5 Toward Shared Benchmark Suites

Finally, the field would benefit from benchmark suites that evaluate multiple regimes on common tasks. The current literature is rich but fragmented. Some papers optimize textual faithfulness, others tool use, others structured traces, and still others executable replay or predictive planning. A strong benchmark suite should support several solution modes and require papers to report which evidence form they expose, how it is checked, and what the cost is. Such suites would not eliminate specialization, but they would make it easier to answer cross-cutting scientific questions such as when structure is worth the cost, when tool use genuinely improves faithfulness, or when world models outperform reactive policies for the right reasons rather than for incidental benchmark artifacts.

### 14.6 Standards for Reporting and Reproduction

The final challenge is cultural as much as technical. Many papers describe their intermediate traces qualitatively but stop short of specifying how those traces should be reproduced, inspected, or stress-tested. As a result, two papers may both claim "better reasoning" while exposing completely different kinds of evidence and relying on incompatible evaluation assumptions. A more mature standard would require authors to report at least the following artifacts: a canonical serialized trace format, the precise verifier or execution environment used for checking, failure cases where the trace looks reasonable but is wrong, and the runtime budget under which the reported behavior is obtained. These requirements are modest, but they would dramatically improve comparability across the field.

Such reporting standards would also help future surveys. At present, a significant amount of synthesis work must be reconstructed from paper-specific descriptions, project pages, or code repositories because papers use inconsistent terminology for very similar behaviors. Standardized reporting would make it easier to understand whether two methods differ in representation, verifier strength, or simply in how much of the evidence pathway is surfaced to the reader. In that sense, better reporting is not only a service to reproducibility; it is part of the scientific infrastructure required for verifiable multimodal reasoning to become a coherent field rather than a loose collection of adjacent ideas.

An especially useful near-term step would be to standardize **paper-level claim templates** for verifiability. Authors should be encouraged to state claims in a form such as: "our method improves step correctness under metric X," "our method improves grounding under perturbation Y," or "our method reduces replay cost under budget Z." This may sound procedural, but it would force much tighter alignment between method design, experimental evidence, and reviewer expectations. In a literature where many systems now expose long intermediate traces, the discipline of stating exactly which property is improved is increasingly part of the scientific contribution itself.

### 14.7 Limitations of This Survey

This survey also has limits that should be stated explicitly. First, the corpus is **structured but expert-curated**, not a fully systematic review with exhaustive database export, screening logs, and inter-annotator agreement. We believe this is the right trade-off for a rapidly evolving, preprint-heavy field, but it means the paper prioritizes conceptual coverage and synthesis over formal completeness guarantees. Readers should therefore interpret the corpus as representative of the main design space rather than as a definitive census of all related multimodal reasoning papers.

Second, the taxonomy deliberately centers **evidence interfaces** rather than architecture families, downstream applications, or benchmark leaderboards. This sharpens the paper's main thesis, but it also compresses differences that may matter in other reviews. Two papers placed in the same quadrant can still differ substantially in model scale, domain assumptions, or interaction budget. Likewise, some hybrid systems inevitably lose nuance when assigned one primary quadrant for reproducibility.

Third, the review is strongest on the methodological core of visual reasoning systems and somewhat lighter on downstream deployment literature in medicine, science, and regulated industries. We discuss these applications to expose the trust implications of different evidence interfaces, but we do not claim to exhaust the application-specific safety, policy, or human-factors literature in each domain. Those areas remain important opportunities for follow-on surveys.

These observations lead to five practical recommendations.

1. **Match the intermediate trace to the verification need.** If the task only requires human-readable justification, Q1 may be enough. If the task requires reproducible evidence, move toward Q3 or Q4.
2. **Do not treat tool use as automatic verification.** Q2 only improves trust when tool selection, grounding, and output interpretation are themselves validated.
3. **Use Q1 and Q2 as data-collection and warm-start layers.** Many successful systems first learn to expose traces in language and only later strengthen them with structured or executable supervision.
4. **Budget for verification explicitly.** Reporting only answer accuracy hides the real system trade-off. Cost, latency, and security are part of the method.
5. **Treat world models as a predictive overlay, not a replacement taxonomy.** The relevant question is what kind of future evidence the world model produces and how that evidence is checked.

To make these recommendations more actionable for future papers, we suggest a minimal reporting protocol. Every paper on multimodal reasoning should make explicit: (i) what intermediate evidence is exposed, (ii) how that evidence is checked, (iii) what kinds of failures remain unchecked, and (iv) what runtime budget is required to obtain the reported performance. This protocol would make it easier to compare a Q1 paper that relies on best-of-N textual traces with a Q4 paper that uses executable replay, or a Q2 agent with a Q3 structured-state method. Without such reporting, the field risks mixing together systems with very different trust profiles under a single label of "reasoning."

## 15 Conclusion

The recent literature shows that visual reasoning is moving from explanation generation toward evidence-centered design. This shift is visible in many forms: better process supervision for textual reasoning, stronger tool grounding in agents, structured intermediates that support schema and alignment checks, executable traces that can be replayed, and predictive world models that evaluate actions before they are taken. What unifies these developments is not a single architecture but a common goal: to make intermediate reasoning inspectable enough that users and downstream systems can decide when to trust it.

Our main conclusion is therefore simple. Verifiable visual reasoning should be studied as a design space defined by **representation**, **verification channel**, and increasingly **predictive lookahead**. No single quadrant dominates across all settings. Textual methods remain the most readable, tool-augmented agents remain the most practical for open-world tasks, structured self-contained methods offer a valuable middle ground, and executable systems provide the strongest replayability when their operational cost is justified. Future progress will depend less on producing longer chains of thought and more on building systems whose intermediate evidence can be checked, challenged, and reused.

From a systems perspective, the most important message of this survey is that trust does not arise from verbosity. Longer rationales, more tools, larger world models, or more elaborate code traces do not automatically yield better evidence. Trust arises when a system exposes intermediate states in a form that matches the application, can be challenged through an explicit verification channel, and remains usable under realistic cost and safety constraints. This is why the most promising research direction is not a search for one universally best reasoning interface, but a disciplined approach to composing textual, structured, tool-grounded, executable, and predictive components into systems whose evidence pathway remains legible from end to end.

If the field succeeds on this agenda, the long-term payoff will be larger than better benchmark scores. Multimodal systems will become easier to debug, easier to regulate, easier to integrate into high-stakes workflows, and easier for users to calibrate correctly. That outcome would mark a real transition from models that merely *sound* thoughtful to systems whose reasoning can be responsibly relied upon.
